About This Unit

On the Road

What does it mean to travel — and to be changed by the going?

This unit starts with a question that sounds simple but keeps getting more complicated: what is a journey? Not just movement from one place to another, but the experience of being changed by going — of leaving somewhere, passing through, arriving, and finding you're not quite the same person who set out.

You'll read across a range of forms that take travel as their subject: poetry, short fiction, memoir, and film. Some of the journeys you study will be physical — people crossing continents, deserts, oceans. Others will be internal: journeys of imagination, of self-discovery, of necessity. The unit asks you to think about what all these different kinds of journeys have in common, and what makes each one distinct.

The central text is Ben Stiller's The Secret Life of Walter Mitty (2013). It's also the focus of your formal assessment. Keep connecting what you learn throughout the term back to Walter Mitty — his physical journeys, his imagined ones, and what the whole experience does to him as a person.

Focus Questions

These questions run through the whole unit. You won't answer them all at once — but keep returning to them as you move through the texts:

  • What makes a journey transformative, rather than just travel from A to B?
  • How do different text types — poetry, memoir, short story, film — represent the inner dimension of a journey?
  • What's the difference between a journey you choose and one that's forced on you?
  • Can you be on a journey without moving at all?
  • What does the destination represent? What does the road itself represent?

Concepts

What Is a Journey?

More than moving from A to B

Physical| Inner| Necessity| Hero's Journey| Imagined

A journey, in its simplest sense, is movement through space. But the texts in this unit are interested in a richer question: what happens to the traveller? The most memorable journeys in literature and film are transformative — the person who arrives is meaningfully different from the person who left. Understanding the different types of journey helps you analyse how texts use travel as a way to explore something deeper.

Physical journey
Literal travel through space — across countries, continents, oceans, or even just across town. The physical journey is often the surface layer of a text, while the real interest lies in what it does to the person making it.
Inner journey
A journey of the mind, emotions, or spirit — a shift in understanding, identity, or values. Most journey texts involve an inner journey that is triggered by or runs parallel to a physical one.
Journey of necessity
Travel that is not a choice — migration, exile, displacement, escape. This kind of journey often carries the heaviest emotional weight in a text because the traveller carries loss as well as hope.
Imagined journey
Travel that happens entirely in the mind: daydreams, fantasies, vicarious experiences. These can be escapist, but they can also reveal deep truths about what a person desires or fears.
Rite of passage
A journey that marks a transition — usually from one stage of life to another. Often involves a test or ordeal that the traveller must survive to claim a new identity.
Spiritual journey
Travel toward something beyond the physical — meaning, purpose, transcendence, or a confrontation with mortality. Often associated with pilgrimage, but the destination doesn't have to be religious.

Physical Journeys

The physical journey is where most travel narratives begin: someone sets out from somewhere and goes somewhere else. But the more you look at great travel writing and film, the more you notice that the physical journey is rarely just about getting from A to B. It's a structure that allows the other kinds of journeys to happen.

Think about why that is. Travel removes you from the familiar. It strips away the routines, relationships, and environments that usually define who you are. When those external anchors are gone, you're thrown back on yourself — forced to discover what you're actually made of. That's why so many journey narratives involve some kind of adversity or obstacle: the desert, the ocean, the foreign city where you don't speak the language. The difficulty is the point. It's the pressure that transforms.

In The Secret Life of Walter Mitty, Walter's physical journeys — to Greenland, Iceland, Afghanistan, Nepal — are the mechanism by which his transformation is made possible. He couldn't have discovered who he was by sitting at his desk in Manhattan. The distance was necessary.

Discussion

Think of a physical journey you've taken — even a short one — that felt meaningful. What changed for you, if anything? What's the difference between a trip that feels like travel and one that just feels like transport?

Inner Journeys

An inner journey is a change in the self — a shift in values, understanding, identity, or emotional state. It's the part of a journey text that you can't photograph. Most serious journey narratives are really about inner journeys; the physical travel is the occasion for them.

Inner journeys typically have a structure: a catalyst that disrupts the traveller's existing way of seeing; a period of difficulty or disorientation; and some form of resolution — not necessarily a happy ending, but a change that has been earned. In The Secret Life of Walter Mitty, the inner journey follows exactly this pattern. Walter's catalyst is the threat to his job and the missing negative; the period of difficulty is the entire global search; and the resolution is his discovery that he was always more than he believed himself to be.

The inner journey is harder to represent on screen or on the page than the physical one — writers and filmmakers need techniques to externalise the internal. Frost does it through the speaker's internal monologue. Thurber does it through comic fantasy sequences. The 2013 film does it through the contrast between Walter's daydream sequences and his real experiences, and through the structural device of the eHarmony dating profile — a literal record of a life's dimensions, filling up as the film progresses.

Task

Identify two techniques used in any of the unit texts to externalise an inner journey — to show, rather than just tell, that a character is changing on the inside. Be specific: what is the technique, and what does it reveal?

Journeys of Necessity

Not all journeys are chosen. Some people travel because they must — because staying put would mean poverty, danger, death, or persecution. These are journeys of necessity: migration, refugee experience, exile, displacement.

What makes journeys of necessity distinctive in texts is the weight of what has been left behind. The voluntary traveller leaves on their own terms and can theoretically return. The person who is forced to travel carries loss — of home, of community, of language, of identity — alongside the hope for something better. The road becomes a space between two worlds, neither of which is fully home.

Journeys of necessity are central to Australian identity. This country was settled by people transported involuntarily, built by successive waves of migration, and shaped by the ongoing experiences of Aboriginal peoples who have been displaced from their Country. When you read travel texts this term, pay attention to the power dynamics underneath the journey: who chose to go? Who had no choice? Who travelled with comfort and money, and who travelled with nothing?

Steve Crombie's Lost on Earth explores journeys of necessity as part of its investigation of displacement and belonging. Keep that dimension in mind as you read Chapter 1.

The Hero's Journey — and Its Limits

You've probably encountered the "hero's journey" — the narrative pattern identified by Joseph Campbell and later adapted for screenwriters by Christopher Vogler. In its basic form: a hero leaves the ordinary world, crosses a threshold into an adventure, faces escalating challenges, reaches the innermost cave, undergoes an ordeal, seizes the prize, and returns transformed. You can fit The Secret Life of Walter Mitty into this template easily enough.

The template is useful — it helps you spot structural patterns across different texts, and it identifies some genuine universals about how stories of transformation work. But use it carefully, because it has real limitations:

  • The hero's journey is originally based almost exclusively on myths featuring male protagonists. It doesn't always fit the experiences of women, children, or people whose journeys aren't about conquest and return.
  • It assumes the journey is chosen and the return is possible. Journeys of necessity — forced migration, exile, displacement — don't fit the model at all.
  • It focuses on the individual hero rather than on what travel does to communities, cultures, or relationships.
  • It can flatten complex, ambivalent travel narratives into tidy three-act arcs when the actual text is more interesting and messier than that.

Think of the hero's journey as one lens among many — helpful when it fits, but not a template you should force every journey text through.

Imagined Journeys

Some of the most interesting journeys in this unit happen entirely inside someone's head. Walter Mitty — both in Thurber's 1939 story and in the 2013 film — is the master of the imagined journey. His fantasy sequences take him to places he'll never actually go: the cockpit of a Navy plane in a hurricane, an operating theatre, a firing squad.

Imagined journeys aren't just escapism. They reveal the gap between a person's inner life and their outer circumstances — the distance between who they are and who they wish they could be. That gap is often the emotional heart of a text. Thurber's story is essentially a comedy of this gap: Walter's fantasies are grandiose and heroic; his real life is shopping trips and parking disputes. The 2013 film takes the same gap and transforms it — Walter actually closes it. His real journeys eventually exceed his imagined ones.

Pay attention to imagined journeys as a textual technique: they tell you what a character desires and fears, often more directly than anything they say or do.

Symbolism

The Symbolism of Travel

What roads, crossroads, and horizons have always meant

Travel is one of the oldest metaphors in human culture. Long before anyone was analysing texts, people were using the imagery of roads, crossroads, horizons, and homecoming to talk about life — about choice, change, death, time, and the self. That depth of cultural resonance is why travel keeps appearing as a subject in literature and film: writers can tap into a symbolic vocabulary that audiences already feel.

Key Symbols and Their Meanings

The road represents life, time, and the direction a person is heading. A road's characteristics carry meaning: a straight road implies certainty and inevitability; a winding road implies uncertainty or mystery; a road that forks or diverges implies a choice that must be made.

The crossroads is the moment of decision — the point at which two or more possible futures open up and you can only choose one. Crossroads have been associated with magic, fate, and irrevocability across many cultures. In travel narratives, the crossroads moment is often when a character's true values are revealed.

The horizon represents aspiration, possibility, and the unknown. Characters who are drawn toward the horizon are drawn toward the future — they're defined by desire and forward momentum. The horizon also carries a hint of melancholy: it's always receding, always just out of reach.

The departure is simultaneously a beginning and a loss. Leaving somewhere means abandoning the familiar, the safe, the known. In texts, departures are often charged with grief — for home, for people left behind, for the self you were before you set out.

The return — homecoming — is one of the most emotionally complex moments in a journey narrative. The traveller who returns is never quite the same person who left, and the home they return to has also changed (or looks different through changed eyes). Homer's Odyssey, the archetypal return narrative, is mostly about the difficulty of getting back — and then the further difficulty of recognition when Odysseus does.

The vehicle or vessel — the ship, the car, the plane — can symbolise the container of the self, a kind of mobile home that both separates you from the landscape and carries you through it. Walter Mitty is almost never without something to carry — the briefcase he hauls across three continents is an interesting case of this symbolism.

Luggage and possessions represent what a traveller carries from the old life into the new. What someone chooses to take, and what they leave behind or lose, is often symbolically significant. In The Secret Life of Walter Mitty, the moment Walter discards the wallet he's been given — not knowing it contains the missing negative — is one of the film's most loaded symbolic actions.

Poetry

The Road Not Taken

Robert Frost, 1916 — the most misread poem in English literature

Robert Frost published "The Road Not Taken" in 1916. In the century since, it has been quoted at graduations, printed on inspirational posters, and invoked as a celebration of nonconformity and individual courage. Almost every one of those uses gets the poem exactly wrong.

Reading it carefully is good practice for the whole unit: it shows you that texts don't always mean what they appear to say at first.

Two roads diverged in a yellow wood, And sorry I could not travel both And be one traveler, long I stood And looked down one as far as I could To where it bent in the undergrowth; Then took the other, as just as fair, And having perhaps the better claim, Because it was grassy and wanted wear; Though as for that the passing there Had worn them really about the same, And both that morning equally lay In leaves no step had trodden black. Oh, I kept the first for another day! Yet knowing how way leads on to way, I doubted if I should ever come back. I shall be telling this with a sigh Somewhere ages and ages hence: Two roads diverged in a wood, and I— I took the one less traveled by, And that has made all the difference. — Robert Frost, Mountain Interval (1916)

Reading the Poem Carefully

The conventional reading of this poem is something like: "I chose the less-travelled path, and my boldness was rewarded." But look at what the poem actually says.

In stanza 2, the speaker describes the unchosen road as "just as fair" and "having perhaps the better claim / because it was grassy and wanted wear." But he immediately qualifies this: "Though as for that the passing there / had worn them really about the same." The roads are not meaningfully different. The speaker cannot honestly say one was more travelled than the other.

Then in stanza 3: "And both that morning equally lay / In leaves no step had trodden black." Both roads are equally untravelled. There is no less-travelled path. The choice is arbitrary.

So why does the speaker say, in the final stanza, "I took the one less traveled by / and that has made all the difference"? Because he will tell himself that — "I shall be telling this with a sigh / Somewhere ages and ages hence." The poem is not celebrating a brave choice. It's describing the story we tell ourselves about our choices after the fact. It's a poem about how humans construct meaning from arbitrary decisions — how we turn the crossroads into destiny once we've already committed to a direction.

The poem's real subject is retrospection, not boldness. The "sigh" in the final stanza can be read as regret, not satisfaction.

Dramatic monologue
A poem spoken by a single speaker to an implied audience, revealing character through what they choose to say (and what they reveal without intending to).
Retrospection
Looking back at the past from the present. Many journey texts are told retrospectively — the narrator already knows the outcome and is making sense of how they got there.
Ambiguity
The quality of a text that allows two or more valid interpretations simultaneously. Frost's poem is deliberately ambiguous: the "sigh" and the "roads equally worn" create a reading that undercuts the poem's own apparent conclusion.
Self-justification
The psychological tendency to construct narratives that make our past choices seem more deliberate and correct than they actually were. The Frost poem dramatises this tendency directly.

Connection to Walter Mitty

There's a resonance between Frost's poem and the 2013 film worth thinking about. Walter Mitty, at the start of the film, is a man who has taken no road at all — whose eHarmony profile, the visible record of his life, is a catalogue of blanks. The film is about what happens when he is forced, by circumstance, to actually make a choice and go somewhere.

Frost's poem asks: do our choices define us, or do we just tell ourselves they do? The film asks something related: what is the cost of making no choices — of keeping the first road for another day, indefinitely?

Non-Fiction

Non-Fiction Travel Writing

The travelogue as a literary form

Travel writing — the non-fiction account of a journey, written for an audience who wasn't there — is one of the oldest literary genres. From the ancient accounts of Herodotus to the modern travel essay, travel writing asks: what do I see, what does it mean, and what does it reveal about me as the observer?

The best travel writing is never just description. It uses the journey as a frame for thinking about culture, identity, history, and the self. The traveller is always both subject and observer — someone who is shaped by what they see, and who inevitably brings their own assumptions and blind spots to what they describe.

Conventions of the Genre

Travel writing has a recognisable set of conventions — ways of doing things that readers expect and that you can identify and analyse:

  • The observational voice — a first-person narrator who notices things carefully and reflects on what they mean. The writer positions themselves as both tourist and witness.
  • Evocative description — sensory detail (sight, sound, smell, texture) that puts the reader inside a place they haven't visited. Strong travel writing makes you feel the heat, hear the traffic, taste the food.
  • The encounter — meeting a local person, stumbling on an unexpected situation, having a conversation that illuminates something about the place or the writer. Encounters are the joints of the narrative: they move it forward and introduce new perspectives.
  • Reflection and significance — the writer pauses to think about what an observation or experience means. This is where travel writing becomes more than journalism: the writer is after something larger than the facts.
  • Voice and tone — travel writing has enormous tonal range, from the comic (Bill Bryson) to the elegiac (Bruce Chatwin) to the urgent and political (Ryszard Kapuściński). The tone shapes everything about how the place and the journey are represented.

Case Study: Bill Bryson, In a Sunburned Country

Bill Bryson's 2000 book about travelling through Australia (published in Australia as Down Under) is a useful case study in travel writing technique. Bryson is an American living in Britain who arrives in Australia as a genuine outsider — he knows very little about the country, is repeatedly astonished by it, and is particularly fascinated (or alarmed) by the number of things in Australia that can kill you.

Bryson's signature technique is comic deflation: he sets up a grand, romantic expectation about a place and then undercuts it with a mundane or absurd reality. His prose is colloquial and self-deprecating — he positions himself as the bumbling tourist rather than the authoritative expert. This creates an ironic gap between the strangeness of what he observes and the ordinariness of his reaction to it.

But there's more going on than just comedy. Bryson is genuinely interested in Australia's history, its peculiar ecology, and what it means to be Australian. The humour is a way of making accessible observations that might otherwise be too direct or confronting — he can say something pointed about Australian indifference to its own history by making himself the joke.

Travelogue
A non-fiction account of a journey, typically written for a general audience. Combines description, reflection, and narrative — the writer is both reporter and character.
Persona
The version of the writer constructed within the text. A travel writer's persona isn't the same as the actual person; it's a carefully crafted narrative voice that serves the text's purposes.
Comic deflation
A technique where grand or romantic expectations are undercut by mundane or absurd reality, creating humour and ironic distance.
The outsider's gaze
The perspective of someone who doesn't belong to a place looking at it from the outside. Can reveal things that insiders take for granted — but also risks misunderstanding and cultural bias.

Writing in the Genre

One of the most effective ways to understand how a genre works is to try writing in it. Travel writing looks easy — you just describe what you saw — but the craft lies in the selection, the voice, and the reflection.

Short Fiction

The Original Walter Mitty

James Thurber's 1939 short story — the source text

Before the 2013 film, there was James Thurber's short story — published in The New Yorker on 18 March 1939. It's only about 2,000 words long. It introduced Walter Mitty to the world, and it's been in the cultural vocabulary ever since: a "Walter Mitty" has become a phrase in English meaning someone who escapes into elaborate fantasies to compensate for a dull reality.

Reading the original story before studying the film is important because it shows you how an adaptation works: what the filmmakers kept, what they changed, and why. The film is not a faithful adaptation — it uses the story as a starting point and then goes somewhere completely different.

The Story

Thurber's Walter Mitty is driving his wife into town to do errands. As he drives, he drifts into an extended fantasy sequence: he's a Navy commander piloting a seaplane through a hurricane, calm and authoritative, his crew relying on him. His wife interrupts him — "You were up to fifty-five," she says, about his driving speed — and he snaps back to reality.

The story proceeds through five fantasy sequences, each triggered by something in Walter's mundane surroundings. In the fantasies, Walter is always heroic, always the person others look to: a brilliant surgeon, a cool assassin on trial, a fearless RAF bomber pilot, and finally — in the story's last image — a man facing a firing squad, "inscrutable to the last."

Between the fantasies, the real Walter is meek, forgetful, hen-pecked, and incompetent. He can't remember what he was supposed to buy at the drugstore. He parks the car in a no-parking zone. He struggles with the snow chains on the tyres. The contrast between inner life and outer reality is total — and deliberately comic.

Thurber's Technique

Thurber moves between reality and fantasy without warning — the transitions are abrupt, and the reader has to do the work of recognising the shift. This mimics the experience of the daydream: the mind slips sideways without announcement.

The fantasy sequences are written in the overblown style of pulp fiction and Hollywood B-movies — the genre conventions of 1930s masculine heroism. "Ta-pocketa-pocketa-pocketa" is the sound of the operating theatre equipment, rendered in comic onomatopoeia. The language of the fantasies is as artificial and overcooked as Walter's reality is flat and grey. The joke is in the contrast between the two registers.

Thurber's story is comic in mode but melancholy in implication. Walter Mitty is a man who has built an entire interior life to compensate for the fact that his exterior life offers him nothing. The fantasies are not a harmless hobby — they're an indictment of the ordinary. The final image, Walter standing alone in the rain, composing himself for an imaginary firing squad, is both funny and sad.

Free indirect discourse
A narrative technique where the narrator slips into the character's perspective without formally marking it. Thurber uses this to blur the line between Walter's reality and his fantasy.
Bathos
A sudden drop from the elevated to the mundane, for comic effect. The transition from Walter-as-heroic-surgeon back to Walter-in-the-car is a bathos effect.
Parody
An imitation of a genre or style for comic or critical purposes. Thurber's fantasy sequences parody the conventions of pulp fiction and Hollywood adventure stories.
Source text / adaptation
A source text is an original work that is later adapted into another form (film, stage, graphic novel). An adaptation transforms the source material — sometimes radically — for a new medium and audience.

Comparing Story and Film

When you study the 2013 film, keep the original story in mind. Here are some key differences to think about:

  • In the story, Walter's fantasies are pure wish-fulfilment and are never realised. In the film, Walter's real experiences eventually match and exceed his fantasies. The film is much more optimistic.
  • In the story, Walter is middle-aged and married. In the film, he is single and yearning — there is a romantic subplot and the possibility of a future.
  • In the story, there is no quest, no job threat, no missing photograph. The fantasy sequences are self-contained and don't drive the narrative toward any resolution. The film adds a plot engine.
  • In the story, Walter never changes. The final image — the firing squad daydream — confirms that he will go on fantasising, unchanged. The film gives him a transformation that the story withholds.

When an adaptation makes significant changes, it's worth asking: why? What does the change reveal about the values of the era in which the adaptation was made? The film's optimistic ending says something about 2013 — about what audiences wanted from a story about the gap between imagined and real life.

Comparative Task

Write a paragraph (150–200 words) arguing that either the story or the film has the more interesting ending. Use evidence from both texts to support your argument.

Travel Memoir

Lost on Earth — Chapter 1

Steve Crombie — journey, displacement, and searching for home

You're studying only Chapter 1 of Steve Crombie's Lost on Earth. This is deliberate: the opening chapter of any memoir is doing particular work. It has to establish the writer's voice, the emotional stakes of the journey, and the central questions the book will explore. Everything that follows depends on what is set up in Chapter 1.

Read Chapter 1 actively — not just to find out what happens, but to notice how it's written. Pay attention to the voice, the structure, the use of place and landscape, and the emotional register. What kind of traveller is the narrator? What are they looking for?

How Memoir Openings Work

The first chapter of a travel memoir typically has to do several things at once: it must establish the narrator's voice with enough distinctiveness that you want to spend a whole book with them; it must create a felt sense of the journey's emotional stakes — why does this journey matter?; it must introduce the central tension or question that will drive the narrative; and it must place the reader physically and emotionally into the world of the text.

One common technique is to open in medias res — in the middle of things, already in motion, already somewhere. This drops the reader directly into the journey's texture before pulling back to explain the context. Another technique is the retrospective frame: the narrator looks back at the beginning from a later vantage point, already knowing how things turn out, which creates dramatic irony (the reader knows something is coming that the traveller-in-the-story doesn't).

Notice which approach Crombie uses, and what effect it creates.

Displacement and Belonging

Travel writing and memoir often circle around questions of belonging: where is home? Where do I fit? What does it mean to be displaced — to be physically present somewhere but not emotionally or culturally rooted there?

For some travellers, displacement is the point — they seek it out, they want to be estranged from the familiar. For others, displacement is imposed: they travel because they have to, and the journey is about trying to reconstruct a sense of belonging in a new place.

As you read Chapter 1 of Lost on Earth, consider where on this spectrum Crombie's narrator falls. Is this a chosen journey or a necessary one? Is the narrator seeking displacement or trying to escape it? What does "lost" in the title mean — geographically, emotionally, existentially?

Close Reading Task

Choose a paragraph from Chapter 1 that you think is particularly well-written. Copy it out, then write an analysis of three to five sentences explaining: what specific language choices Crombie makes, what effect those choices create, and what they reveal about the narrator's relationship to the journey.

Discussion

Based on Chapter 1 alone, what do you expect the rest of the book to be about? What question or tension has been set up that needs resolution? What kind of person do you think the narrator will become by the end of the journey?

In medias res
"Into the middle of things" — a narrative technique that begins the story at a point of action or movement, without lengthy preamble.
Retrospective narration
A narrative told by a narrator who is looking back at events from a later point in time. Creates irony because the narrator knows more than the character they were at the time.
Displacement
The state of being removed from one's familiar context — physically, culturally, or emotionally. A recurring theme in travel memoir and migrant literature.
Voice
The distinctive personality of the narrator as it emerges through language choices — vocabulary, sentence rhythm, what they notice, what they care about, how they describe things.

Try to apply at least one of the opening techniques discussed above: in medias res, or the retrospective frame.

Main Film Text

The Secret Life of Walter Mitty

Dir. Ben Stiller, 2013 — journey, identity, and the life unlived

The Unlived Life| The Quest| Greenland| The Journey Continues| Real Life| Quintessence| Themes & Techniques

Ben Stiller's The Secret Life of Walter Mitty is not a simple film. It's a big-budget Hollywood movie with Iceland landscapes, a romantic subplot, and a rousing soundtrack — and it's also a genuinely thoughtful exploration of the unlived life: what it costs to keep yourself safe, small, and imaginary, and what it feels like to discover you are capable of more than you knew.

The film works on multiple levels. On the surface, it's a comic adventure about a meek photo-negative manager who travels the world. Underneath, it's about the gap between the person we are and the person we imagine ourselves to be — and whether that gap can ever be closed. It earns its optimism; by the time the ending lands, Walter has genuinely changed, and you've been shown exactly why.

Study the film attentively. Watch it at least twice if you can — once for the story, once for the technique.

Walter Mitty · Part 1

The Unlived Life

Act One — who Walter is before anything changes

The film's first twenty minutes are a careful, almost ruthless portrait of a man who has stopped living. Everything in Act One is designed to show us the shape of Walter's unlived life — not by telling us he's dissatisfied, but by showing us the architecture of his existence and letting us feel its weight.

The Checkbook Entries

We see Walter's checkbook early in the film. The entries tell his story more economically than any dialogue could: his mother's rent; his sister's dental braces — and Walter is 36 years old. The checkbook is a portrait of a life organised entirely around obligation to others. Walter has no line item for himself. His existence, financially and emotionally, is structured entirely around taking care of his family.

This is the first and most important thing to understand about Walter: he is not a selfish person who needs to learn generosity. He's a generous person who has forgotten — or never knew — that he is also entitled to a life of his own.

The eHarmony Profile

Walter is trying to connect with a woman named Cheryl on the eHarmony dating site. His profile has a section called "Been There Done That" — the space where you list your life experiences. Walter's section is empty. The eHarmony customer service representative, Todd Mahar (played by Patton Oswalt), is bewildered: "That column right there, where it's blank? That's the hard part. That needs to have something in it."

The empty profile is the visible shape of the unlived life. It's not just that Walter has never done anything exciting — it's that he has no story to tell about himself. In a culture that values experience and adventure, Walter has nothing to show. He is, as his eHarmony counsellor tells him with gentle brutality, "indistinguishable from everybody else."

The eHarmony thread is one of the film's most elegant structural devices. It runs throughout the entire narrative: as Walter travels further and does more, the profile gradually fills with real experiences — Greenland, the Atlantic, Iceland, Mogadishu, Nepal. The profile becomes a record of a life being lived in real time, and Walter's growing ability to talk to Cheryl is a measure of his transformation.

The Time-Life Corridor

Walter works in the photo-negative department of Life magazine, in the Time-Life Building in Manhattan. His daily walk to his desk takes him through a corridor lined with enormous photographs: K2, the Normandy beaches, extreme expeditions. The world's most dramatic moments, on a massive scale, surrounding Walter as he carries his coffee and his briefcase to his desk.

This is the film's central irony in visual form: Walter is the person responsible for preserving and transmitting images of the world's most extraordinary experiences, but he has never had one himself. He processes adventure without living it. The corridor is the gap between Walter's inner life and his outer one made architectural.

The Daydream Sequences

Walter's fantasy sequences are spectacular — he imagines himself as an action hero, a romantic lead, a figure of Olympian courage. They're shot and edited with the full energy of a Hollywood blockbuster, and they're funny. But they're also sad, because every daydream is interrupted by the mundane reality Walter can't escape from.

Crucially, the daydreams never go anywhere. Walter never acts on them. They are a relief valve, not a preparation — a way of tolerating his real life rather than changing it. This is what makes him different from the Walter Mitty of the 2013 film's arc, who eventually learns to act rather than just imagine.

Walter Mitty · Part 2

The Quest and the MacGuffin

What Walter is looking for — and what the search is really about

The plot of The Secret Life of Walter Mitty is driven by a missing photograph. The film's plot engine is simple: Sean O'Connell, the magazine's reclusive and legendary photographer, has sent a batch of film negatives to be processed for the final issue of Life magazine. He tells Walter in a letter that negative 25 is "the quintessence of life" and should be the cover. But negative 25 is missing.

The MacGuffin

The missing negative is a classic narrative device: the MacGuffin — a term coined by Alfred Hitchcock to describe the object that everyone in the story is pursuing, but whose specific nature doesn't really matter. What matters is the pursuit, not the thing itself.

In The Secret Life of Walter Mitty, the MacGuffin is the missing negative 25. Walter needs it because the new management of Life (represented by the smug, beard-rated Ted Hendricks, played by Adam Scott) is about to shutter the magazine's print edition — this will be the last-ever physical issue — and Hendricks is threatening to fire Walter if he can't produce it.

So Walter sets out to find Sean, who photographed the missing negative and presumably knows where it went. This is the inciting incident that pushes Walter out of his desk and into the world.

MacGuffin
A plot device — an object, goal, or piece of information that motivates the characters and drives the narrative, but whose specific value to the audience is secondary to the journey it provokes.
Inciting incident
The event that disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and sets the main story in motion. For Walter, it's the discovery that negative 25 is missing and his job is threatened.
Stakes
What the protagonist stands to lose if they fail. Stakes are what make an audience care about a narrative. Walter's stakes are his job, his livelihood, and implicitly — given what the film is really about — his last chance to become who he could be.

What the Quest Is Really About

The search for the negative is the film's surface plot. But the deeper quest — the one the film is really interested in — is Walter's search for himself. Going out into the world to find Sean is also going out into the world to find out who Walter is when his familiar structures of obligation and routine are stripped away.

This is the hero's journey at work: the quest object is never just the quest object. In Walter's case, what he's ultimately searching for is evidence that he is capable of living a real life. The negative, when found, will confirm this — or destroy the possibility entirely. The stakes of the MacGuffin and the stakes of the inner journey turn out to be the same thing.

Pay attention to the Life magazine motto that appears throughout the film. It reads: "To see the world; things dangerous to come to; to see behind walls, draw closer, to find each other and to feel. That is the purpose of Life." This mission statement is Walter's moral compass for the journey — and an implicit indictment of the person he has been.

Task

In two to three paragraphs, explain how the search for negative 25 works on two levels: as a plot device (the MacGuffin) and as a metaphor for Walter's inner journey. What does the missing negative represent beyond itself?

Walter Mitty · Part 3

Greenland: The First Real Journey

Walter leaves the building — and enters the world

The clue that sends Walter to Greenland comes from a waterlogged overexposure in one of Sean's negatives — a glimpse of a ship's hull with the word "Erkigsnek." Walter traces it to a supply vessel operating out of Nuuk. He buys a ticket. He packs a bag. He goes to Greenland.

The moment Walter realises he's actually going is beautifully observed. He says the word "Greenland" to himself — quietly, experimentally — like he's testing whether a world of physical adventure can actually include a person like him. It's one of the film's most resonant small moments.

The Mundane Made Extraordinary

In Nuuk, Walter hires a cab. The driver is a cheerful, voluble woman who turns out to be extraordinarily capable. In the course of the trip, Walter discovers that she's also the Queen of Greenland. "You have a queen?" he asks. "Of course," she says, as if this is obvious — and then she tells him which bar Sean probably went to.

This moment — the extraordinary embedded in the mundane — is a pattern that repeats throughout the film's international sequences. The world Walter travels through is not the world of his daydreams, where every setting is spectacular and every person is heroic. It's a world where remarkable people do ordinary things, and ordinary things turn out to be remarkable. The contrast with his fantasies is deliberate: real life, the film argues, is richer than imagination if you just pay attention to it.

The Leap

To reach the ship Erkigsnek, Walter has to jump from a helicopter into the North Atlantic. The pilot is drunk. The helicopter is lurching. Walter, still carrying his briefcase, has to make a decision in seconds.

He jumps.

This is the film's first significant act of physical courage — the first moment where the Walter of the real world does something that matches the Walter of the daydreams. The briefcase goes with him. He swims to the ship. He's let aboard.

The briefcase is worth noticing. Walter carries it through every subsequent country — Greenland, Iceland, Afghanistan, Nepal. It's never fully explained, but it functions as a kind of tether to his old life: the professional, obligated, office-bound Walter carries this object through every adventure. By the time it's finally lost — in the chaos of Los Angeles — Walter no longer needs it.

The Freighter Dolores

On board the ship, Walter finds a clue: one of Sean's handwritten itineraries, stuffed in with the crew's supplies. The itinerary traces Sean's movements across the globe. Walter is given cake by Chilean stevedores who make him feel, for the first time, genuinely welcomed by strangers. He marvels at the informality, the warmth — "small world," one of them says, and Walter agrees, though what he means is the opposite: the world is suddenly, dizzyingly larger than he knew.

The Greenland sequence establishes the emotional pattern that will repeat throughout the international sections: Walter, in an unfamiliar place, is surprised to find himself capable of more than he thought.

Task

The helicopter jump is the film's first major act of real courage. Write a paragraph analysing how the scene is constructed — the sequence of shots, the decision point, the aftermath. What does the scene establish about how Walter is changing? What role does the briefcase play in this scene?

Walter Mitty · Part 4

The Journey Continues

Iceland and Mogadishu — obligation, freedom, and the life filling up

The itinerary from the freighter leads Walter to Iceland, and then — after an interruption that sends him back to New York — to Mogadishu.

Iceland: The Double Pull

Walter reaches Reykjavik by an unexpected route: he kayaks through a triathlon when his hire car breaks down. He paddles through the event without stopping because stopping isn't an option. By the time he reaches the city, he has completed most of a sporting event he didn't sign up for. This is a pattern: Walter's real experiences keep accidentally outdoing his fantasies.

In Iceland, Walter learns that Sean has moved on — there's an erupting volcano, and Sean went toward it. But before Walter can follow, he gets a call from home: his mother's piano needs to be moved. His sister's show needs to happen. Obligation — the original architecture of his unlived life — pulls him back.

Walter returns to New York. For a moment it seems like the journey is over, like the old structure has won. But something has shifted: the experiences in Greenland and Iceland have lodged in him. He can't go back to exactly who he was.

Mogadishu: The Third Journey

The next clue takes Walter to Afghanistan. The catalyst this time is the Life magazine motto — the mission statement that appears on the wallet Sean gave Walter as a gift. "To see the world, things dangerous to come to…" — Walter reads it and goes.

He finds Sean at a makeshift football pitch surrounded by Korean soldiers. He's missed him by seconds — Sean has just been airlifted out. But the missing is not failure. Walter skateboard through Mogadishu, spontaneously, impossibly freely — and the eHarmony profile picks up another entry. Real Mogadishu, experienced in person, goes into the column that was empty at the beginning of the film.

The Mogadishu sequences are shot with a looser, more improvisatory quality than the Greenland and Iceland scenes — there's a feeling of genuine spontaneity, of Walter moving through the world without a script. This is the film's visual language for transformation: as Walter changes, the cinematography changes with him.

The eHarmony Thread

Throughout all of this, the eHarmony thread continues. Walter calls Todd Mahar from airports and mountain paths to update his profile. Each call marks a new stage of the journey. Todd, who started as a comic presence, becomes something more: a witness to Walter's transformation, a voice that acknowledges what he's becoming.

By the time Walter reaches Nepal, he no longer needs the calls — because he no longer needs to construct a narrative about his life. He's living it.

Task

Trace the eHarmony thread through the film. In the first scene, the "Been There Done That" column is empty. What entries fill it as the film progresses? What does the completed profile represent at the end of the film? Write two paragraphs analysing the eHarmony thread as a structural device.

Walter Mitty · Part 5

"Real Life": The Meeting with Sean

Nepal — the quest completed, the lesson given

Walter finally meets Sean O'Connell in the mountains of Nepal. Sean is camped in a forest, waiting for snow leopards — one of the most elusive creatures on earth. There are only a handful left in the wild. Sean has been waiting for days.

The Meeting

When Walter reaches the camp, he carries two things he knows he needs to do: find negative 25, and ask Sean about the photograph that was supposed to be the cover of Life's last issue.

Sean's answer is simple. He gave Walter the wallet as a gift — a real wallet, the kind you carry your whole life — and he put the photograph in it. "That was the gift," Sean tells him. "The picture. The wallet was just someplace to keep the picture. I put it in the little picture holder."

Walter understands immediately. He discarded the wallet on the flight to Nepal, when his faith in the journey faltered — when he thought, briefly, that the institutional purpose of Life magazine wasn't worth what the journey was costing him. He threw away the thing he was looking for without knowing he had it.

The Snow Leopard

But Sean doesn't react to this revelation with alarm or frustration. Instead he turns Walter's attention to the forest. A snow leopard — a ghost cat, barely real — is approaching the clearing. Sean has his camera ready, but he doesn't raise it.

"When are you going to take it?" Walter asks.

"Sometimes," Sean says, "I don't." Some things, the film suggests, are too real to capture. Some moments exist fully only in the present tense, not in images or archives or magazine covers. To photograph them would be to turn them into something else — into a record, a product, a story you tell later.

Then he looks at Walter and says, simply: "Real life."

Two words. That is Sean's entire philosophy. Real life is this: a snow leopard in a clearing, two men watching in silence. It is not the fantasy sequences. It is not the empty eHarmony profile. It is not the obligation architecture of the checkbook. It is the present moment, fully inhabited.

What Sean Represents

Sean O'Connell, throughout the film, is almost mythical. He appears in glimpses, is present just before or just after Walter arrives, lives in inaccessible places. He is the photographer who captures the world at its most alive — who is always where things are most real. He is, in the film's moral architecture, what Walter could become: a person who doesn't need fantasy, because they've discovered how to fully inhabit reality.

The relationship between Walter and Sean is not one of direct mentorship — they barely interact until Nepal. But Sean's example operates as a kind of gravity throughout the film, pulling Walter toward the life he has not yet lived.

Walter Mitty · Part 6

The Quintessence of Life

The final reveal — the quest object was always himself

The film's resolution turns on a discovery that recontextualises everything that has come before. When the final issue of Life is published and Walter sees the cover, he understands what Sean photographed and why it was "the quintessence of life."

The Cover Photograph

The cover shows Walter at his desk in the Time-Life Building — alone, absorbed in a contact sheet of negatives, in a state of total concentration. It's a photograph taken without his knowledge, sometime before the film's events began. Sean was in the building. He saw something. He took the picture.

What Sean saw was a man completely present in his work — not fantasising, not performing, not filling an empty profile column. Just doing the thing he was made to do, with his whole attention. Walter, the person the film has spent ninety minutes showing us was "indistinguishable from everybody else," turns out to be the quintessence of life. He was the thing Sean was looking for.

The Quest Object Was Always Present

This is the film's deepest structural irony: Walter spends the entire film searching for negative 25, travelling to Greenland, Iceland, Afghanistan, and Nepal — and the photograph on that negative is of Walter himself, in his natural element, before the journey began. The thing he was looking for was always with him. The quest was never about finding an external object. It was about becoming the person capable of recognising what was already there.

Compare this to the Frost poem: the road not taken is the road you construct after the fact, in retrospect, to make sense of where you've ended up. Walter constructs his transformation through the journey, and then discovers that the destination was always at the beginning. The circular structure is deliberate.

The Transformation Is Permanent

The film's ending shows Walter with Cheryl, now in a relationship. The eHarmony profile is long completed. The magazine is closed — the institution that defined his identity is gone — but Walter is not diminished by its absence. He has an identity of his own now, one that doesn't depend on the Time-Life Building, the checkbook, or the empty profile column.

The final daydream sequence — if there is one — is gone. Walter no longer needs to imagine a better version of himself. He is that person now.

Walter Mitty · Part 7

Themes and Techniques

What the film is about, and how it says it

Theme: Identity and Authenticity

The film's central theme is the gap between performed identity and authentic selfhood. Walter's pre-journey self is entirely defined by what he does for others — his obligations, his job, his social performance. His inner life is rich but private, expressed only in daydreams that he never acts on. He has no public identity of his own.

The journey forces him into situations where performance is impossible — where he can't play the meek office worker, because there's no office, there are no colleagues, and the only thing that will get him through is what he actually is. The Greenland helicopter jump doesn't give him a new identity; it reveals an existing one that obligation and routine had buried.

The film asks: what is the relationship between who we are and the life we actually live? Can you be an authentic person while living an inauthentic life? Walter's answer, worked out over five countries, is no.

Theme: Imagination vs. Reality

The film's attitude toward Walter's imagination is nuanced. It doesn't simply celebrate imagination as a gift or dismiss it as escapism — it does both. The daydream sequences are visually extravagant and clearly enjoyable, but they're also symptoms of stasis. Walter uses imagination as a substitute for action, not as a spur to it.

The crucial shift in the film's second half is that reality starts to exceed the fantasies. When Walter swims through icy Atlantic water to a ship, or kayaks uninvited through a triathlon, or skateboards through Mogadishu, these events are filmed with the same cinematic energy as the daydream sequences — but they're real. The film is arguing that the world, fully inhabited, is more interesting than any dream of it.

This is a pointed critique of a certain kind of modern escapism: streaming services, social media, screen time — all the ways contemporary life lets you be a spectator of other people's experiences without having your own. Walter's initial condition is recognisably modern.

Theme: Purpose and Institutional Meaning

Life magazine is dying throughout the film. The last print issue is being assembled; the digital future has won; the institution that gave Walter his identity for sixteen years is closing. This is not incidental background — it's the film's third major theme.

Walter has defined himself through his relationship to Life and its mission: "to see the world, things dangerous to come to." But he has lived the institutional version of this mission — maintaining the archive, preserving the negatives, managing the process — without ever living it personally. The magazine's closure forces the question: who are you when the institution you belonged to no longer exists?

The answer the film provides is: the person you always were, before the institution arrived to define you.

Technique: The Fantasy / Reality Contrast

The film's most important technical device is the contrast between Walter's fantasy sequences and his real experiences. The fantasies are shot in saturated colour, with theatrical lighting, operatic music, and the genre conventions of Hollywood action and romance. The real world of Walter's office life is desaturated and fluorescent-lit.

As the film progresses and Walter's real experiences become more extraordinary, the visual language of reality and fantasy begins to converge. Iceland is as beautiful as anything in the daydreams. Afghanistan and Nepal are shot with the same epic quality. The visual distinction between inner and outer dissolves — which is the film's way of showing you that Walter has dissolved it in himself.

Technique: Soundtrack and Music

The film's score and soundtrack are carefully deployed. Songs appear at key moments of transition: music marks Walter's departures, his arrivals, and his moments of decision. Pay attention to the relationship between lyric content and image — the songs are often ironic, counterpointing the action rather than underscoring it.

The sound design also distinguishes the fantasy sequences from reality: the daydreams have a heightened, slightly artificial quality; the real world sounds have texture and immediacy.

Technique: Cinematography and Landscape

The film was shot in Iceland, Greenland, and other locations, and the landscape is not incidental. Walter's first experience of genuine natural grandeur — the volcanic Icelandic terrain, the Greenlandic coastline — is filmed with awe. The landscapes dwarf Walter in a way that the Time-Life corridor's giant photographs never quite did, because this time he's actually there.

The camera frequently places Walter small within the landscape: a lone figure on a vast glacier, a speck against a mountain range. This is a visual argument about scale and significance — Walter, tiny in the frame, is nonetheless fully present in a world that is immeasurably large.

Genre Study

Road Trip Tropes

The recurring conventions of journey stories — and what they mean

A "trope" is a recurring pattern in a genre — a plot device, character type, or situation that appears so frequently it has become a convention audiences recognise and expect. Tropes are not clichés, exactly: a skilled writer can use a familiar trope in a fresh, meaningful way. But tropes become clichés when they're used thoughtlessly, without the writer being aware of what the pattern usually means.

The road trip genre has a rich set of tropes. Learning to identify them helps you analyse how specific texts use, subvert, or comment on the conventions of the genre.

Common Road Trip and Journey Tropes

The pair or group. The solo traveller and the travelling companions are both archetypal, but they function differently. Solo travel is about confronting the self without the buffer of social performance. The travelling pair or group introduces relationship dynamics: conflict, dependency, revelation of character. The journey tests the relationship as well as the individual.

The breakdown or obstacle. The vehicle breaks down, the road is washed out, the plan falls apart. The obstacle is never just an obstacle — it's a forcing function that makes characters reveal who they really are. How people respond to things going wrong is character-defining information.

The unexpected detour. The traveller ends up somewhere they didn't plan to go — and it turns out to be exactly where they needed to be. The detour is one of the road trip genre's most optimistic tropes: it argues that the unplanned journey is sometimes the real one, and that getting lost is a legitimate form of navigation.

The stranger who helps. Someone the traveller didn't expect, in a place they didn't expect, provides exactly what they need. Sometimes practical help; sometimes wisdom; sometimes just kindness. The stranger who helps is often a minor character who functions as a catalyst — they say or do one thing that changes the trajectory of the journey.

The point of no return. A moment when the traveller can still turn back, but doesn't. After this point, the journey is committed, for better or worse. In The Secret Life of Walter Mitty, the helicopter jump is a point-of-no-return moment: Walter can refuse and go back to his desk, or he can jump. He jumps.

The final destination that isn't the real destination. The traveller sets out toward a geographic goal, but what they actually needed to find was something internal — a self, a relationship, a truth. The destination turns out to be symbolic. This is the trope that underlies almost all journey narratives, from Homer to Walter Mitty.

The return. Coming home changed. The returning traveller is no longer who they were, and the home they return to is the same but looks different through new eyes. The hardest part of the return is often the recognition — being seen, and seeing yourself, clearly for the first time.

Exam Preparation

Past HSC Short Answer Questions

Practice questions — what the exam asks for and how to respond

The HSC exam for English Studies includes short-answer questions that require you to analyse specific features of a text — language choices, structural devices, the effect of particular moments or techniques. They're not essay questions; they want focused, precise responses that demonstrate that you've actually looked closely at the text.

What markers reward is evidence: not generalisations ("Walter Mitty uses many effective techniques"), but specific references to moments in the text, named techniques, and clear explanation of effect. Get in the habit of writing short responses that have this structure: name what's happening → quote or describe the specific moment → explain the effect on the reader/viewer.

For a full collection of past HSC short-answer questions for this topic, visit the class site: Past HSC Short Answer Questions [Google Site]

How to Structure a Short-Answer Response

For a typical 4–6 mark short answer question, aim for three to four sentences of focused analysis. A reliable structure:

1. State what the text does. Identify the technique or strategy being used.
Example: "The film uses a visual contrast between Walter's saturated daydream sequences and the desaturated lighting of his office environment to…"

2. Refer to the specific moment. Ground your analysis in a concrete scene, line, or image.
Example: "In the opening sequence, as Walter imagines himself as a Navy pilot, the colour palette shifts dramatically from grey to deep blue and gold, while…"

3. Explain the effect. What does this technique do to the reader/viewer? What does it communicate?
