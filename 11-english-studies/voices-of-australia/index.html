About This Unit

This unit starts with a simple question: who gets to speak, and how? Australia doesn't have a single, unified culture — it has many, layered and sometimes in tension. The voices that get amplified in media, literature, and public life are not always representative of the country's actual diversity. Whose stories get told? Whose don't?

You'll work across three text types — slam poetry, a stage play, and advertising — each using different tools to give voice to Australian experience. You'll analyse how writers and performers from diverse backgrounds use language, form, and context to shape meaning. And you'll produce your own work: an original slam poem to write and perform, and a group advertising campaign for a real Australian play.

The central texts are Melanie Mununggurr-Williams's Double Threat (slam poetry) and Finegan Kruckemeyer's The Violent Outburst That Drew Me to You (stage play). Supporting performances by Alice Eather, Luka Lesson, and Laurie May give you a broader picture of what Australian slam poetry looks and sounds like.

Week 1

What Makes a Voice Australian?

Introduction to slam poetry and the idea of representation

Bankstown Poetry Slam|Apologies to Vegetarians [YouTube]|Peter Drew street art [NAA]

Who's Speaking?

Write five words you associate with Australia. Now look at what you wrote: who does that image include? Urban or rural? Indigenous or settler? Young or old? Recent migrant or third-generation? The gap between the official image of Australia and the lived experience of its many communities is exactly what this unit explores.

Australia doesn't have one voice — it has many. But not all of them get equal airtime. When we ask what makes a voice Australian? we're also asking: whose voices tend to be missing? Texts — poetry, plays, advertising — always make choices about representation: who is shown, in what light, from whose perspective.

Look at the Aussie Aussie Aussie: Peter Drew street art project and watch Apologies to Vegetarians. As you look and watch: whose Australia is this? What's being celebrated, and what's being left out?

Two Australian Voices

Here are two very different takes on Australian identity — one from 1908, one from 1964. They're both "Australian voices" in the sense of being widely known and influential, but they say very different things about what Australia is.

I love a sunburnt country, A land of sweeping plains, Of ragged mountain ranges, Of droughts and flooding rains. I love her far horizons, I love her jewel-sea, Her beauty and her terror — The wide brown land for me! — Dorothea Mackellar, My Country (1908)

Now read this, from Donald Horne's The Lucky Country (1964):

Australia is 'a stupid society, a childish place that is self-confident with the familiar and uneasy with the unfamiliar, not capable of reacting to danger or making its own decisions'. The image of Australia is of a man in an open-necked shirt solemnly enjoying an ice-cream. He 'likes to sit in the sun and say nothing, do nothing and think very little'. Cleverness can be considered un-Australian. It is 'a nation without a mind', with 'triumphant mediocrity'. Much energy is wasted in pretending to be stupid. To appear ordinary, just like everybody else, is sometimes a necessary condition for success in Australia. — Horne's argument, as described by Ingeborg van Teeseling (2026), who migrated to Australia in 2006

Mackellar's poem is lyrical, loving, and about the land. Horne's critique is urban, satirical, and about the culture. Neither is the "whole truth" — but together they show that even the question of what Australia is has never had a single answer. What does it mean that both of these count as "Australian voices"? Who do they include and exclude?

What is Slam Poetry?

Slam poetry is a competitive, performance-based form that emerged in Chicago in the 1980s and became a major vehicle for voices outside the mainstream — particularly from working-class, migrant, and Indigenous communities. It's spoken aloud, usually without props or backing track, and judged by the audience. A slam poem is not written to be read: it's written to be heard.

What makes slam distinctive:

  • Rhythm and repetition. These create momentum and emphasis the way music uses beats and hooks. A repeated line accumulates weight each time it returns.
  • Personal story. Almost always grounded in specific, lived experience: a moment, a relationship, a place, a name. Not abstract ideas — real, particular things.
  • Shifting tone. The emotional register can move quickly — anger to humour to grief. Good slam earns those shifts; they're not random.
  • Performance. The poem is completed in the room. Pauses, volume, eye contact, movement are not extras — they're part of the meaning.

Watch 2–3 performances at the Bankstown Poetry Slam. For each one, hold two questions in mind: what is this poem about on the surface, and what is it really about underneath? Where does it hit hardest, and how does the poet make that happen?

Annotation task

Choose one Bankstown Poetry Slam performance. Annotate it for rhythm, repetition, tone shifts, and images that stand out. For each technique you identify, write one sentence about what effect it creates.

Show, Don't Tell

The most important craft principle for your slam poem — and for almost all good writing — is this: show, don't tell.

Telling states an emotion directly: I was angry. Showing creates the conditions for the reader or listener to feel that emotion without being told to: I bit the inside of my cheek until I tasted copper.

Sensory detail is your main tool: what you can see, hear, smell, taste, touch. The more specific, the better. "A summer day" slides past people. "Cicadas screaming in the she-oaks, the smell of sunscreen on hot skin" takes you somewhere. Vagueness is forgettable; specificity catches.

Finding Your Moment

Your poem begins with one specific, real moment. Not "my identity" or "my culture" in the abstract — those are topics, not starting points. A starting point is: a time you felt proud, ashamed, excluded, or powerful. A moment where you realised something. A small, quiet thing that stayed with you. A time you were treated unfairly, or stood up for something.

Once you have the moment, make it vivid and concrete. Write:

  • what you saw
  • what you heard
  • what you felt physically and emotionally

Useful starters: I remember…  ·  The room smelled like…  ·  My hands felt…  ·  Everyone was… but I was…

Slam Poem — Draft 1

Write 8–10 lines about your moment, using specific sensory detail. Read it aloud — does it sound like you? Share with a partner. Give each other one specific comment on what worked and one genuine question about something you want to know more. Then expand into a longer draft.

Week 2

Context, Culture & Double Threat

How context shapes meaning — and writing analytically with IDEA

Double Threat — Melanie Mununggurr-Williams [YouTube performance]

Melanie Mununggurr-Williams and Double Threat

Melanie Mununggurr-Williams — Double Threat, 2018 Australian Poetry Slam Champion [YouTube performance]

Melanie Mununggurr-Williams is a Yolŋu woman from Arnhem Land. Double Threat was written from a specific, urgent place: she had just given birth to a Black daughter, and the poem is driven by fear for what that daughter will face — the compounding disadvantages of being born both Indigenous and female. "Two strikes" names what society puts against you: statistical inequalities in education, incarceration, and life expectancy, and the normalisation of sexual violence. The title seems, at first, like a verdict. By the end, it's been reclaimed.

The poem is fierce and at moments tender, not funny. Its power comes from a combination of raw statistics (“I'm a statistic that says you'll get a degree before me”) and personal emotion — a mother's fear for her daughter. There is irony, but it's specific and deliberate: the moment where she names the expectation that a "good black girl" stays quiet, and then answers it with a single word: "Wrong!" Irony here is not a comic technique — it's a rhetorical one, used to set up a rebuttal.

Notice also that the poem is addressed to an implied listener — "you're born with no strikes / and I'm born with two" — a privileged white male presence who never speaks. This direct address creates the dynamic of the poem: she is speaking at someone with power, not just expressing herself into the air.

Read the transcript below, then watch the performance. The transcript tells you what the poem says; the performance tells you what it does.

Black woman — don't want to be just another black woman standing up here talking about black women's issues and how black women are seen as second-rate citizens in this country because of these two reasons. But here I am. Because when I gave birth to a black girl this year I realised how important it was that she grew up in a world without fear. You see, black women are already birthed into society fighting for equality because of the colour of our skin and of the genitals we're born with — born with two strikes against us from the very beginning, while white boys with privilege arrive already winning. It's a race neither of us has entered into, yet you're born with no strikes and I'm born with two. Strike one: I'm born with a target on my back — it's a target of default simply because I'm born black. I'm a statistic that says you'll get a degree before me, and while your babies will strive and thrive, educated well throughout their lives, mine will fight to survive — if their lives aren't first stolen by suicide. I'm a statistic that puts me in prison before you. I'm a statistic that says I will die sooner too. Strike two: Girl — targets all over her. My heart breaks to think that men will always try to fuck her, and chuck her, hit her and quit her — wolf whistle, catcall, grope and abuse her. And this behaviour is acceptable because she's a girl. This behaviour is normalised in our messed-up world. She becomes one in five black women sexually assaulted or threatened, and because she bears the heirs to your thrones, then her pay wage is lesser. Now, I try not to be political, I try to keep my mouth shut — after all, that's the role of a "good" black girl, right? Wrong! We might be born with two strikes, but we're far from out. Black women all over are an uprising nation — a force to be reckoned with, to be seriously taken. Our resilience comes from deep within, from being born with two strikes from the very beginning. But turn the page and see how the ink has bled through from the blood of black women born with two strikes too. Their strength, their legacy, their fight lives on — two strikes couldn't stop them. Black women are a weapon Mother Earth kept secret. So, on that note, let me introduce you to — Black woman. Double threat. — Melanie Mununggurr-Williams, 2018 Australian Poetry Slam Champion

Key Vocabulary for the Unit

These terms will come up across all the texts this term — though not all of them apply equally to every text. Resilience is central to Double Threat. Codeswitching and diaspora fit more naturally onto Alice Eather, Luka Lesson, and Vivien Pham — poets and writers navigating between cultural worlds linguistically and socially. Use each concept where it genuinely fits rather than applying them all to every text.

Resilience
The capacity to maintain or recover one's sense of identity and dignity under sustained pressure. Central to Double Threat's second movement.
Stereotype
A fixed, oversimplified image of a group that flattens individual difference. In Double Threat, the poem confronts the statistical systems that enforce stereotypes rather than just naming them.
Belonging
The feeling — or struggle — of being accepted and at home in a community or place. Relevant across most texts in the unit, but in different ways.
Codeswitching
Shifting between languages, dialects, registers, or cultural behaviours depending on context. More directly relevant to Eather's Yuya Karrabura, Luka Lesson's work, and Pham's Coconut Children than to Double Threat.
Diaspora
A community living away from its traditional homeland, maintaining cultural ties across distance. Relevant to migrant and refugee experiences; does not apply to Mununggurr-Williams, who is Yolŋu on Country.

Analysing Double Threat: the IDEA Structure

When you write analytically about a text, you need to connect an idea to evidence to technique to effect. The IDEA structure gives you a reliable scaffold:

LetterWhat it meansIn practice
Idea What is the text doing at this moment? State the central claim of your paragraph.
Detail (Quote) Specific textual evidence Quote briefly and precisely — not half the poem.
Examination (Technique) What language or poetic device is at work? Name and explain the technique — don't just label it.
Analysis (Effect) What does this create for the audience? Describe the emotional, intellectual, or political effect.

As you read Double Threat, annotate for:

  • Extended metaphor — "strikes" structures the entire poem, not just a line. How does the metaphor get built up, and how is it transformed at the end?
  • Direct address — who is the "you" in "you're born with no strikes"? What does speaking directly at this implied listener do to the poem's tone?
  • Evidence and emotion — the poem alternates between citing statistics and speaking as a mother. Why both? What does each do that the other can't?
  • Tone shifts — where does the poem move from anger to tenderness to defiance? The shift around "My heart breaks to think..." is worth close attention.
  • The turn — find the single word that pivots the poem's argument. What does it do, and how does the poem's final image ("a weapon Mother Earth kept secret") complete that turn?

Some paragraph starters to get you writing:

The poet shows that [idea]. This is shown in the line, "[quote]." The poet uses [technique]. This [analytical verb: emphasises / highlights / exposes] the idea that [expand]. This makes the audience [feel / understand / question] that [effect].

Or more directly: Mununggurr-Williams critiques / challenges / represents [X] through [Y]. This is evident in "[quote]." The use of [technique] [what it does]. This effectively [foregrounds / reinforces / interrogates] [idea], prompting a deeper understanding of [theme].

Analytical paragraph

Write one IDEA paragraph responding to: How does Mununggurr-Williams use language to turn the poem's central metaphor on its head? Or: How does she construct resilience as a response to injustice? Use a specific quotation and name the technique clearly.

Your Poem: Finding Your Message

Your slam poem needs a clear central message — not just a topic, but something you actually want to say. Before drafting further, nail down your intentions:

  • My poem is about ___
  • My message is ___
  • I want my audience to feel ___
Slam Poem — Draft 2

Rewrite 4–6 lines to strengthen your central message. Ask yourself: does every line earn its place? Does every line connect to what you're really trying to say? Revise the full poem, then work through it in a small group conference.

Week 3

Introducing the Play / Strengthening Imagery

A third Australian voice — and figurative language in your poem

Violent Outburst — Finegan Kruckemeyer [playwright's page]|Education Pack: Part A · Part B [PDFs]

The Violent Outburst That Drew Me to You

The Violent Outburst That Drew Me to You — Finegan Kruckemeyer [playwright's page] · Education packs: Part A · Part B

Finegan Kruckemeyer is one of Australia's most produced playwrights for young audiences. The Violent Outburst That Drew Me to You centres on Connor, a teenager dealing with anger, isolation, and a complicated sense of where he fits. The world of the play is recognisably Australian — suburban, a bit bleak, punctuated by dark comedy and unexpected moments of real tenderness.

The play uses voice very differently from slam poetry. In slam, a poet speaks directly to the audience — there is an "I" and a "you", and the relationship between them is the whole point. In drama, we overhear characters who speak to each other, not to us. What they reveal is often what they don't say directly — we have to infer, to read between the lines, to notice what's being avoided.

As you read the opening scenes, track:

  • Who are the characters, and what do the first scenes tell us about them?
  • Where are they? What's the atmosphere of the world they inhabit?
  • What pressures shape Connor? What is he angry about — and is his anger always directed at the right thing?

Stage Directions, Dialogue, and Subtext

Plays communicate through two main channels: what characters say (dialogue) and what the playwright tells actors to do (stage directions). But beyond both of these is subtext — the meaning that lives underneath the words, in what characters avoid saying, the emotions they suppress or misdirect.

Read stage directions carefully — they're not decoration. A direction like (long pause) or (turns away without answering) tells you as much about a character as any line of dialogue. The pause is a choice: something is being held back, and it's your job to notice what.

Strengthening Your Poem: Imagery and Metaphor

Metaphor and symbolism are how poetry creates meaning beyond the literal. A metaphor doesn't just describe something — it reframes it, asking you to see one thing through the lens of another. The effect is compression: you can say in one image what would take a paragraph to explain directly.

To use metaphor to express emotion, think about what the emotion physically is or does. Instead of I felt trapped, write the trap: every conversation a wire I kept walking into. The concreteness does the emotional work. Avoid clichés ("walls closing in", "heart of stone") — they've been used so many times they no longer land. Find the image specific to your experience.

Slam Poem — imagery revision

Add 2–3 new metaphors to your poem draft. Share with a partner and ask: what image stayed with you? Use that response to decide which images to keep and which to replace.

Week 4

Comparing Texts / Performance Skills / Advertising

How different forms handle the same themes — and how to perform your poem

Double Threat [YouTube]|Violent Outburst — Finegan Kruckemeyer [playwright's page]|BPS poster examples [image search]

Comparing Double Threat and the Play

Both texts explore what it means to belong — or not belong — in Australian society. But they use very different forms, and form is not neutral: it shapes what can be said and how.

  • Slam poetry uses direct address: the poet speaks straight at you, in the first person, in the present tense of performance. Drama requires you to observe and infer — characters speak to each other, not to us.
  • A slam poem is usually one voice, one perspective, one sustained act of speaking truth. A play has multiple voices in dialogue, and truth emerges from their collision and tension.
  • In slam, body and voice are the whole instrument. In drama, staging — physical space, blocking, the relationship between bodies — also carries meaning.

Think about: how does each text represent identity? What emotions does each explore, and through what techniques? Where do they overlap, and where do they diverge?

Written response

Write an IDEA paragraph comparing one specific moment in Double Threat with one moment in the play. Focus on how each text represents Australian experience through its chosen form. Alternatively: answer How does the play represent Australian youth? in a focused paragraph with a specific scene reference.

Slam Performance: Finding Your Voice in the Room

A slam poem on the page is a script. In performance you have more tools — use them deliberately.

  • Volume: vary it. A sudden drop to a near-whisper can be more powerful than shouting. If everything is loud, nothing is.
  • Emphasis: stress the words that carry the weight of meaning — not every word, not every line.
  • Pauses: silence is not empty. A pause after a key line gives the audience time to feel it. Don't rush past your best moments.
  • Eye contact: don't look over their heads. Look at actual people in the room. This is what makes a performance feel like a direct conversation rather than a recitation.

Rehearse with a partner. Homework: memorise or near-memorise your poem.

Poetry Slam event — Wednesday 20 May, 1pm

There is a live Poetry Slam event this week. It is not assessed — no grade, no rubric — but it's a real audience, and that changes everything. Bring whatever draft you have. Performing before you're completely ready often tells you more about what the poem needs than another round of editing does.

Understanding Advertising: Persuasion and Multimodal Texts

Advertising is designed to change how you feel about something and move you to act. It does this through a combination of visual, verbal, and emotional techniques working simultaneously — it's a multimodal form, using image, text, colour, typography, sound, and layout all at once.

Look at a range of theatre posters, radio ads, and social media reviews. For each, identify:

  • the target audience — who is this actually made for?
  • the hook or slogan — what single idea does it lead with?
  • the tone — cool and minimal? urgent? warm and inviting? provocative?
  • the persuasive technique — emotional appeal? urgency? social proof? prestige?

Sample material: Bankstown Poetry Slam poster examples [image search]

Advertising for a theatre production is different from advertising a product. The play already has content, characters, themes — your job is to represent it honestly while making it irresistible to a specific audience. The campaign must be true to what the play actually is.

Campaign planning

As a group, agree on your key message, visual identity (colours, feel, imagery), and tone. Then begin drafting your poster: what does the eye read first? What does the slogan do? What information is essential?

Week 5

Editing Your Poem / Drafting Your Campaign

Good writing is rewriting — and a campaign is more than its individual parts

Violent Outburst — Finegan Kruckemeyer [playwright's page]|Education Pack: Part A · Part B [PDFs]

Editing Your Slam Poem

By now you should have a full draft and a clear sense of your message. Edit for impact:

  • Cut what doesn't earn its place. If removing a line doesn't change the poem's meaning or emotional arc, it doesn't belong. Redundancy kills impact.
  • Strengthen your opening. Slam audiences decide in the first few lines whether they're listening. Don't warm up — begin in the middle of something real.
  • Strengthen your closing. The last line is what the audience carries out. It should feel inevitable — the only way the poem could end. Make it land.
  • Read it aloud every time you edit. A poem that looks fine on the page can be impossible to perform. Your ear catches what your eye misses.
Finalise for rehearsal

Cut unnecessary lines, strengthen your opening and closing, and read through in a small group. Incorporate feedback and finalise your poem for the rehearsal circle.

Drafting Your Advertising Campaign

The campaign is a package — not four separate tasks, but a coherent set of materials with a unified visual and verbal identity. Before you design anything, agree as a group on:

  • Key message — what one thing do you want your audience to take away?
  • Visual identity — what colours, fonts, and images feel right for this particular play?
  • Tone — comic? urgent? tender? provocative? All of the above?

Poster. Think about visual hierarchy — what does the eye hit first? The image should tell you something about the play without telling you everything. The slogan should be short, memorable, and specific to the emotional world of the play.

Radio ad script. 30–60 seconds. There are no visuals: every word and sound effect carries the full weight. Draft a script that communicates something real about the play without spoiling it. Read it aloud and time it. Cut ruthlessly. Think about how to open (hook the listener in the first five seconds) and how to close (leave them wanting to see it). Refer to the conventions of radio advertising document (distributed in class).

Social media review. Write a short, punchy review that sounds like something a real person would actually post. Decide which platform it's for — a tweet is not an Instagram caption is not a Facebook post — and match the tone to the platform. It should feel genuine, not like an ad, even though it is one.

Merchandise / program design. Design one item (keyring, mug, t-shirt, badge) that captures the world of the play in a single image or phrase. Also draft a performance program including cast, synopsis, and themes — think about what an audience needs to know before they sit down.

Week 8

Deeper into the Play: Character, Conflict, Voice

How drama represents identity and belonging

Violent Outburst — Finegan Kruckemeyer [playwright's page]|Education Pack: Part A · Part B [PDFs]|ABCListen recording|MTC programme

Returning to The Violent Outburst That Drew Me to You

By now you've written and performed your own slam poetry and built an advertising campaign around the play. Time to return to the text itself with fresh eyes — and to bring what you've learned about voice and persuasion to bear on a different form entirely.

The three text types in this unit each represent identity differently:

  • Slam poetry — direct address, personal voice, the body as instrument, speaking at an audience
  • Advertising — persuasion, audience targeting, visual and verbal design working in concert
  • Drama — characters speaking to each other, meaning made through dialogue, inference, and staging

Drama is the form most interested in what is not said. Characters rarely announce exactly what they feel — they show us through how they speak, what they avoid, when they go quiet. As an audience, we are overhearing a world.

Entering the World of the Play

Kruckemeyer is drawn to young people who don't fit neatly — who are angry, disconnected, or hurting in ways the adults around them struggle to reach. Connor is not a simple character. He is difficult and sympathetic at the same time; his anger is real, but it's not always aimed at the right things.

Read the opening scenes and come to class ready to discuss:

  • Who is Connor? What do the first scenes establish about him?
  • Who is Lotte, and what does her relationship with Connor reveal about both of them?
  • What's the setting and atmosphere? What context does the play establish around youth, family, isolation, and community?

Before reading: spend a moment predicting. Given what you know about the play's themes — anger, adolescence, belonging — what kind of world do you expect to find?

Character and Conflict Through Dialogue

Re-read the Connor and Lotte scene. If possible, read it aloud with someone — the act of performing dialogue, even roughly, reveals things the page can't: the rhythm of the exchange, where the silences fall, where the energy suddenly shifts.

Annotate the scene for: insults, humour, tension, awkwardness, and moments of unexpected vulnerability. These tonal qualities are not decorative — each one tells you something about the characters and their relationship.

When you analyse dialogue, you're asking: what does the way this character speaks tell us about who they are? Some starter frames:

  • "Connor sounds ___ because he says ___."
  • "The playwright reveals Connor's ___ through ___."
  • "Lotte's response to ___ suggests that ___."
Written response

Write an IDEA paragraph analysing what one specific exchange between Connor and Lotte reveals about belonging or isolation. Use a short, precise quotation and name the technique — word choice, tone, the rhythm of the dialogue, or use of silence.

Stage Directions and Subtext

Drama is the art form most concerned with what is not said. Stage directions — even minimal ones like (pause) or (she doesn't look at him) — carry enormous weight. They tell actors and readers what the spoken text alone can't: what's being suppressed, what's being felt, what's being decided in silence.

Dialogue
The spoken words between characters — the surface layer of meaning.
Stage directions
The playwright's instructions about movement, gesture, tone, and physical action.
Pause / beat
A moment of silence that carries meaning: something is being felt, suppressed, or decided.
Gesture
A physical movement that reveals or conceals a character's inner state.
Subtext
The emotional content beneath the dialogue — what a character feels but doesn't directly say.

Read a short extract and highlight every stage direction. For each one: what do we learn that the dialogue alone doesn't tell us? Try the model: "The playwright uses the stage direction ___ to show ___."

Discussion: what do we learn about a character that is never directly stated? This is subtext working — and recognising it is one of the most important skills for reading drama.

Performance Workshop: Bringing a Scene to Life

In your group, you'll be assigned a short scene (6–10 lines). Read it through once, flat and straight — no performance choices, just the words. Then read it again making deliberate choices about tone, pace, pauses, and movement. What changes between the two readings? What becomes clearer the second time?

Focus on: tone (what's the emotional register of each line?), pace (where does the scene need to breathe?), pauses (where does silence land and why?), movement (what physical choices reveal character?). Perform twice, then get feedback from another group: what changed? What was clearer?

Adapting Drama for Radio

In radio drama there are no visuals — no stage, no gesture, no movement. Everything comes through sound: voice, dialogue, and sound effects. This is a significant constraint, but also a clarifying one: it forces you to think hard about what words and voice alone can carry.

Take your group's scene and adapt it for radio:

  • Where do you need narration or description to replace what staging would show?
  • Where can a sound cue — a door, footsteps, ambient noise — carry meaning instead of words?
  • How do you handle silence and pause when there's nothing for the audience to look at?

Rehearse your audio version and perform it for the class. As you listen to each other: what helped you understand what was happening? What emotions came through clearly?
